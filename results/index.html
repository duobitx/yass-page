Results Summary

EDFS vs TUS — what the experiments show

Across UC1–UC5 the YASS simulator compared EDFS (an IPFS / bitswap-based decentralized store) against TUS (resumable point-to-point upload, modelling the current operational downlink). EDFS buys resilience and favourable scaling — it relays content through surviving peers, survives producer failure, and delivers faster as the constellation grows — at roughly an order-of-magnitude higher resource cost. TUS is competitive only in the small, fault-free, direct-downlink regime, where it is far lighter.

Headline findings

  • Resilience (UC4): when the producing satellite is destroyed before any ground contact, EDFS relays the photo around the dead node and delivers it (100% wherever a relay peer exists, n ≥ 2, high/default priority), while TUS delivers 0 of 15 variants.
  • Latency (UC1): at small constellations both engines land the file in ~800 s; EDFS pulls ahead only at scale.
  • Cost: EDFS peak RAM ~180–260 MiB vs TUS ~13–15 MiB (≈13–15×), with much higher CPU and network transmit.
  • Priority & EDFS-only UCs: priority ordering is weak/unobservable in these runs; UC3 and UC5 (EDFS-only, no TUS baseline) delivered in every variant, with per-photo completeness in UC5 gated by replication factor.

Caveats bounding the above: EDFS network-TX is an upper bound (~4.46× exporter inflation); receive-side RX is unrecoverable; several large-roster runs have Prometheus metric-extraction gaps; and the TUS-at-scale failures partly coincide with shared-cluster pressure (warrant an independent re-run).
